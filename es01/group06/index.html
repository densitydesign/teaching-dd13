INTRO

 

Over the past 30 years, significant findings regarding global warming highlighted the need to curb carbon emissions.The main cause of global warming is the increased emission of so-called greenhouse gases, in particular, carbon dioxide (chemical symbol CO2).
Human activities since the beginning of the Industrial Revolution (taken as the year 1750) have produced a 40% increase in the atmospheric concentration of carbon dioxide

SOURCE: EPA, United States Environmental Protection Agency

 

 

TIME FOR CHANGE

Most of the observed warming is due to carbon emission. The majority of the world's man-made carbon emissions are released by the burning fossil fuels to create electricity. Fossil fuels are high in carbon when burned, high-carbon energies (such as oil, coal and natural gas) produce major amounts of carbon emission.
Low-carbon power (such as wind power, solar power, hydropower and nuclear power)comes from processes or technologies that, produce power with substantially lower amounts of carbon dioxide emissions. But nowadays high-carbon energy remains the dominant source of energy.

SOURCE: IPCC,IEA

THE PERCENTAGE INCREASEMENT OF ENERGY CONSUMPTION

Internationally, the most prominent early step in the direction of low carbon power was the signing of the Kyoto Protocol, under which most industrialized countries committed to reduce their carbon emissions. OVERVIEW ON TRENDS IN GLOBAL LOW-CARBON ENERGY (LCOE) IN 2016.

The levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) is a measure of a power source which attempts to compare different methods of electricity generation on a consistent basis.For years, many debates on the future of the electricity system centered on getting the balance right between higher costs and lower environmental impacts. The cost of renewable energy has dropped a lot in recent years and now (2016) at competitive levels with other forms of energy.

However, the price remains too high for the amount of energy produced. Hydropower and Geothermal wind in terms of cost are better than other low carbon-energy, but nuclear energy has price stability.

SOURCE: IRENA,LAZARD

WHO IS BUILDING NEW REACTORS?

Today, the majority of the world’s reactors are concentrated in three broad regions: the eastern US; Europe and Asia. For some, the technology has fallen out of favor. European nuclear reactors are shut down gradually. Lithuania and Italy have shut down all of their reactors. Germany’s are being phased out. In contrast, Asian countries plan to increase the nuclear power plants to reduce reliance on dirty fossil fuels.

 

SOURCE: IAEA

DISTRIBUTION OF AGE OF REACTORS

The age distribution varies widely in each region. In terms of generating capacity, the world’s top nuclear powers are the US, France, China, Russia and South Korea. However, their age profiles differ markedly. US reactors are the world’s oldest, with an average age of 36. Those in former Soviet states and the EU average around 31 years. Over the past 20 years, Asia has been the leading light of the nuclear industry. China has the world’s youngest fleet, with an average age of less than eight.

 

SOURCE: IAEA

 

CONCLUSION

Our energy consumption has increased year by year. The use of low-carbon energy is an important means to mitigate climate change.Nuclear energy is a non-polluting, long lasting, high output energy source relative to space taken up by the other low carbon energies. One of the main disadvantages is the difficulty in the management of nuclear waste and nuclear accidents . It takes many years to eliminate its radioactivity and risks.The advantages and disadvantages of nuclear energy have made this energy source one of the most controversial on the market today.
